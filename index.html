Model Answers

Expected Questions

Here are the model answers for the Definitions and Discussion questions. These are crafted to be comprehensive enough for a PhD-level "Discuss and Define" exam, using the specific terminology and examples from the book.

Section 1: Model Answers for Definitions

  • Big ‘C’ Conversations Definition: A "Big ‘C’ Conversation" refers to public debates, arguments, motifs, issues, or themes that circulate widely within a society or social group (e.g., debates over abortion, smoking, or affirmative action). Unlike everyday conversations between individuals, these are metaphorical conversations that exist historically and institutionally. Gee describes them as the way "Discourses use us humans" to talk to and against each other. To understand a text fully, one must know which "Big C Conversation" it is responding to.
  • Intertextuality Definition: Originally a literary term derived from Bakhtin, Kristeva, and Barthes, intertextuality refers to the way texts refer to, allude to, or quote other texts. In Gee’s framework, it has a broader meaning: it includes the mixing or juxtaposing of different Social Languages (distinctive styles of language associated with specific Discourses) within a single text. For example, a speaker might mix "academic language" with "everyday vernacular" to achieve a specific social effect.
  • Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) Definition: Studies how language, as part of social practices, helps give or deny people status, power, and access to social goods.
    • Example: Gee’s history sentence about Whigs and Tories: CDA asks how the wording makes elite control of politics sound normal and hides non-elite struggles.
  • Social mind frameworks Definition: Shared “typical stories” that link social experience to the expectations in our minds about what is normal.
    • Example: “Is the Pope a bachelor?” The dictionary says yes, but our framework of “bachelor” (ordinary, marriage-eligible man) says no.
  • Schema Definition: A mental structure for a familiar situation that organizes what we expect to happen.
    • Example: Restaurant schema: you expect menu → order → eat → pay, even in a restaurant you have never visited before.
  • Cultural models Definition: Shared beliefs and values about how the world normally works and how people ought to behave.
    • Example: A cultural model of morality: “A person is moral if they behave well in private life,” even if they support unjust public policies.
  • Figured Worlds (Cultural Models) Definition: A concept developed by Dorothy Holland, Figured Worlds are simplified, often unconscious theories, stories, or models about how the world works that we use to process information efficiently. They are "socially and culturally constructed realms of interpretation" where specific characters and acts are recognized and valued (e.g., the "Success Model" in the US). They function as a tool of inquiry that mediates between the "micro" level of social interaction and the "macro" level of institutions.
  • Situational Meaning vs. Basic Meaning Definition: Basic Meaning is the general, dictionary-style definition of a word (e.g., "Coffee" is a beverage). Situational Meaning is the specific meaning a word takes on in a specific context of use, negotiated by participants on the spot. For example, in the context "The coffee spilled, get a broom," the situational meaning of "coffee" is grains or beans, not liquid. Discourse analysis focuses on how context shapes situational meaning.
  • Thematic Meaning Definition: A third level of meaning (deeper than Basic or Situational) that connects a text to the core themes, values, and identities of a culture or institution. It is often implicit and built through recurring patterns or motifs (e.g., repetition, parallelism). In the example of Leona’s story, the "home-baked cake" carried the thematic meaning of kinship, while the "bakery cake" carried the thematic meaning of commodity.
  • Reflexivity Definition: The "magical" property of language where it simultaneously reflects the context (what is physically or historically present) and constructs the context (how the participants view the situation). It is like two mirrors facing each other. For example, speaking casually to a colleague reflects the fact that you are friends, but it also constructs the interaction as "friendly" in that moment.
  • Multimodality Definition: The use of different modes of communication (such as words, images, music, gesture, gaze, and movement) within a single text or interaction to create meaning. According to Gunther Kress, a "mode" is a socially and culturally shaped resource for making meaning. In multimodal texts (like video games or face-to-face conversation), different modes interact to provide context and grounding for one another.
  • Sensual Constructions Definition: A concept introduced by Gee to describe multimodal symbols that blend abstract meaning with embodied sensation. They are experienced clusters of sensation and feeling that accumulate deep situational meaning over time. Examples include the Madeleine cookie in Proust (which triggers involuntary memory through taste) or the Red Scarf in Attack on Titan (which visually symbolizes safety, loyalty, and loss).
  • Habitus Definition: A term from sociologist Pierre Bourdieu referring to one's habitual, embodied way of being in the world. It includes ingrained dispositions, tastes, and ways of acting that signal one's social class or status (e.g., knowing how to act in an elite school without thinking about it). In discourse analysis, narratives are often used to rationalize or construct a person's habitus (e.g., Brian explaining how he learned to suppress emotion to fit in).
  • The Frame Problem Definition: The theoretical dilemma that context is indefinitely large (ranging from local eye gaze to global history). The "problem" is that any new aspect of context we consider could change our interpretation of the text. However, Gee argues this is also a "tool": analysts must widen the "frame" (context) until the interpretation stabilizes, and use the widening process to see what information the speaker is leaving unsaid or effacing.
  • Co-Construction (of Identity) Co-construction is the principle that Socially Situated Identities are not solitary performances but are mutually built through interaction. An identity is not just claimed by a speaker; it must be ratified by the listener. In Chapter 15, Gee demonstrates that the Interviewer plays a crucial role in constructing the subject's identity. For instance, the same interviewer constructed a "Global/Abstract Voice" for a College Professor by asking theoretical questions, but constructed a "Local/Personal Voice" for a Middle School Teacher by asking concrete, local questions. Thus, the identity in the transcript is a joint product.
  • Motifs Motifs are recurring themes, images, or ideas that "co-locate" (correlate) throughout a text, giving it texture and coherence. By tracing these threads, an analyst can uncover the speaker's underlying Figured World. In Chapter 16, Gee traces three motifs in Sandra’s interview: Disconnection (actions lacking consequences), Not Caring (indifference as defense), and Language/Laughter (using noise/affect to resist authority). These motifs reveal her worldview that "authoritative representation" is hurtful, while sympathetic interaction is safe.
  • Topic vs. Comment This distinction refers to the information structure of a sentence, which dictates Perspective.
    • Topic (Subject): The entity the sentence is about (the starting point).
    • Comment (Predicate): What is said about the topic.
    Relevance: Writers choose the Topic to frame reality. In the headline "A white nationalist was bloodied," the nationalist is the Topic, creating a perspective of victimhood. If the writer chose "Protestors" as the Topic ("Protestors bloodied..."), the perspective would focus on their agency/aggression.
  • Asserted vs. Assumed Information This refers to the "Social Contract" established by clause structure regarding what is open to debate.
    • Asserted Information: Information in the Independent Clause. It is foregrounded and is the specific claim the speaker asks the listener to accept or reject.
    • Assumed Information: Information in the Dependent/Subordinate Clause. It is backgrounded and treated as taken-for-granted. Writers can manipulate this to force readers to accept controversial premises (e.g., "Trump was shouted down when he was telling the truth" forces the reader to accept the truth-telling as undebatable background fact).

Section 2: Model Answers for Discussion Questions

Q1. Explain Gee’s concept of Big “C” Conversations.

Model answer For Gee, Big “C” Conversations are broad, long-running public discussions about major social issues and themes, such as “race,” “gender equality,” “climate change,” or “immigration.” They are not single face-to-face talks, but ongoing networks of texts and interactions: news reports, social media posts, political speeches, everyday arguments, slogans, and so on, all responding to and reshaping each other over time.
When we speak or write, we often implicitly position ourselves in relation to one or more Big “C” Conversations, even if we are not consciously aware of their history. A simple comment like “All lives matter” cannot be understood only as a literal sentence; it also indexes a specific position in a larger Conversation about racism and social justice.
Big “C” Conversations matter because they connect micro-level language use to macro-level social debates and struggles over values, identities, and power. Gee wants analysts to see that even ordinary-looking utterances may be moves in these bigger Conversations.

Q2. Explain what Gee means by intertextuality and heteroglossia.

Model answer Intertextuality is the idea that any text or stretch of talk is woven out of other texts. Speakers and writers quote, echo, parody, or allude to earlier words, images, and stories. For Gee (drawing on Fairclough), you cannot fully understand an utterance like “Make America great again” or “All lives matter” without recognizing the earlier texts and slogans it reuses and reacts to. Meaning depends on those links to prior discourse.
Heteroglossia (from Bakhtin) is the notion that a single language contains many different social “voices” and styles: legal language, youth slang, bureaucratic jargon, religious speech, academic prose, and more. These voices represent different social groups, values, and perspectives, and they coexist, overlap, and sometimes conflict within the same language.
Together, intertextuality and heteroglossia highlight that language in use is never neutral or isolated. Every text is filled with other people’s words and with social voices, and discourse analysis must attend to these multiple voices and links if it wants to understand how meaning and power are being negotiated.

Q3. Explain Gee’s view of context and the reflexive relation between language and context.

Model answer For Gee, context includes everything that can matter for interpreting an utterance: the physical setting, objects, prior talk, the participants and their relationships, institutional roles, histories, and wider social practices. However, context is not a static backdrop that exists first and then determines language. Instead, the relation between language and context is reflexive.
On one side, context shapes language: being in court vs at a café will normally affect what counts as appropriate talk. On the other side, what people say actively builds and reshapes the context. By choosing certain words, grammatical forms, and social languages, speakers highlight some elements as relevant and push others into the background. For instance, saying “this duck is annoying me” in a cluttered room makes the duck a focal part of the context; before the utterance, it was just one object among many.
Gee argues that interpreting language is the process of following these cues to construct “the context” the speaker is treating as relevant. Because of reflexivity, we cannot separate language from context: language both reflects and constructs the very situation in which it occurs.

Q4. Explain the difference between basic meanings and situational meanings, and relate this to the Frame Problem.

Model answer Basic meaning is the relatively stable, dictionary-like meaning of a word or phrase, abstracted away from particular situations. For example, the basic meaning of “duck” is a kind of bird (or toy), and the basic meaning of “alive” is something like “not dead.”
Situational meaning is what that word or phrase actually means in a specific context, once it is combined with a particular activity, participants, intentions, and background knowledge. “It’s alive” can mean different things in a horror movie, a biology lab, or a conversation about “when life begins.”
The Frame Problem is the difficulty of knowing which aspects of the huge surrounding context are relevant for interpreting an utterance. In real life, there is an unlimited amount of possible information (objects, histories, relationships), but speakers and hearers must somehow select only what matters for the meaning at hand. We cannot pre-list all the relevant features for every possible situation.
Gee uses this to argue that understanding language in use always involves moving from basic meaning to situational meaning by solving the Frame Problem on the spot. People rely on social practices, figured worlds, and shared expectations to decide what to treat as relevant and how to “fill in” the meaning of what is said.

Q5. Explain Gee’s concept of figured worlds and their relation to mental simulations in the mind.

Model answer Figured worlds (or cultural models) are simplified story-like models of “how things normally go” in a particular domain of life: for example, a figured world of “good parenting,” “school success,” or “the American Dream.” They include typical characters, actions, timelines, values, and expected outcomes. These worlds are socially shared, often taken for granted, and can be quite different across groups.
Gee links figured worlds to mental simulations. When we think, plan, or interpret language, we do not just manipulate dictionary definitions; we run little mental “movies” or “games” that simulate events. When someone hears “wedding,” they mentally simulate a scenario based on their figured world of what a wedding is like. Gee distinguishes prototypical simulations (the default, typical scenario) from special-case simulations (modified scenarios when something is unusual).
These simulations are guided and constrained by figured worlds. For example, working-class men in his example may have a lived practice that centers family, but still mentally evaluate themselves using a middle-class figured world of “career success.” In that way, figured worlds and simulations shape how people understand situations, judge themselves, and decide what is possible or appropriate.

Q6. Explain why Gee says that “all meaning is local” and how this relates to figured worlds and politics.

Model answer When Gee says “all meaning is local,” he means that there is no single, context-free meaning for words and expressions. Instead, meanings are always tied to particular communities, practices, and situations, and to the figured worlds those communities live by. Different groups can use the same word—such as “freedom,” “family,” or “democracy”—but plug it into different simulations and narratives, so the practical meaning is different.
Because figured worlds encode assumptions about what is normal, good, and valuable, they are inherently political. They allocate “social goods” like status, respect, and opportunities. A dominant figured world, such as the “American Dream,” can become a yardstick by which people judge themselves, even if their own lives and values do not actually match it. This can create internal conflict (for example, valuing family but still feeling like a “failure” because one does not fit the career-success narrative).
Conflicts over meaning are therefore not just semantic; they are disputes over whose figured world—and whose distribution of social goods—will be treated as legitimate. That is why meaning is both local and contested.

Q7. Explain why, for Gee, transcription is an interpretive and analytic act rather than a neutral record.

Model answer For Gee, transcription is never a simple, objective “recording” of speech. To create a transcript, an analyst has to make many decisions: what to include (only words, or also pauses, overlaps, gestures, intonation), how to break the flow into units (lines, stanzas, turns), and which conventions to use. Each of these decisions reflects theoretical beliefs about what features of the talk matter for analysis.
Gee also points out that speech is produced in spurts or idea units that match speakers’ moment-by-moment focus of consciousness, and that English uses intonation and stress to mark new information. If a transcript leaves out prosody or segments the talk differently, it can hide crucial aspects of the speaker’s meaning. In his “light” example, a child’s reasoning was misunderstood partly because the researcher did not attend to pitch and focus stress.
So a transcript is already a theoretical representation of the data, not raw data itself. To transcribe is to analyze: you are deciding what counts as relevant and shaping how others will see the interaction.

Q8. Explain what makes a discourse analysis “ideal” and “valid” in Gee’s view.

Model answer Gee calls an “ideal” discourse analysis one that would fully and coherently connect fine-grained linguistic details to the broader social practices, identities, and Discourses at stake. It would show, in a systematic way, how specific choices in wording, grammar, and prosody build significance, identities, relationships, politics, and so on. In practice, no analysis completely reaches this ideal, but it functions as a guiding standard.
Because all discourse analysis is interpretive, validity is not about accessing a pure, objective reality. Instead, Gee treats validity as trustworthiness, judged over time by a community of analysts. He highlights four key elements:
  • Convergence: Different analytic tools or questions (e.g., about identity, politics, connections) point toward the same interpretation.
  • Agreement: Other competent analysts or “insiders” to the Discourse find the interpretation plausible.
  • Coverage: The analysis helps explain not only the specific data examined but also related cases (for example, what probably happened before or after).
  • Linguistic detail: Claims are tightly grounded in observable language features, not just vague impressions.
Under this view, a discourse analysis is “valid” to the extent that it satisfies these criteria and remains open to revision as new data and perspectives appear.

Q9. Explain what Gee means by lines and stanzas, and how they help organize information in narratives.

Model answer In Gee’s approach to spoken narratives, a line (also called an idea unit or tone unit) is a small stretch of speech that expresses one main piece of information and is typically produced under a single intonation contour. Lines often correspond to where a speaker might naturally pause or shift pitch.
Stanzas are groups of lines that belong together in terms of time, place, event, or viewpoint. A stanza usually focuses on one small scene or sub-event in the story. Different stanzas can contrast or parallel each other, creating patterns that help listeners see connections and themes.
Looking at talk in terms of lines and stanzas reveals how speakers structure information: which details are introduced as new (marked by focus stress), which are treated as given, how scenes build toward a crisis and resolution, and how parallel episodes invite comparison. Gee’s analysis of African American oral narratives, for example, shows a clear macro-structure and equivalence logic, where two episodes (like breaking a fan and breaking a table) echo each other to express a thematic point. This challenges stereotypes that oral stories are “disorganized” and shows they have their own rigorous poetic and structural logic.

Q10. Explain Gee’s view of grammar as a system of choices (e.g., topic–comment, agent, asserted vs assumed) and how this relates to different levels of meaning.

Model answer Gee treats grammar not mainly as a set of correctness rules but as a system of choices that speakers use to take perspectives on events. Among the most important are:
  • Topic vs comment: Choosing what to put in subject/topic position sets the viewpoint. “The protesters bloodied the man” takes the protesters as the anchor; “The man was bloodied” centers the man as victim.
  • Agent: Grammar can highlight or hide agency. Active voice names who did what; passive voice can omit the agent entirely (“The window was broken”).
  • Asserted vs assumed information: Information in main clauses is presented as asserted, open to challenge; information in subordinate clauses or presuppositions is treated as background and taken for granted.
These grammatical choices are tied to levels of meaning. Basic meaning comes from the literal words and structures. Situational meaning arises when those forms are interpreted in a particular context. Thematic meaning emerges when patterns of grammatical choice across a text connect to wider themes, identities, and histories—for example, systematically backgrounding certain agents or centering particular victims.
Gee’s comparison of human reading and AI (ChatGPT) illustrates this. An AI may handle basic and some situational meanings well, but miss thematic meanings tied to forms of life—such as how a story about a cake can symbolically oppose kinship and commodification. Grammar is one of the main resources through which speakers and writers build those deep, thematic meanings and invite the reader to adopt a particular perspective on social reality.

Chapter 6

Conversations and Intertextuality

Part 1: Key Definitions (For "Define" Questions)

Memorize these definitions, including the scholars' names where applicable.

  • Big “C” Conversations: These are public debates, arguments, motifs, issues, or themes (like abortion, smoking, or affirmative action) that play out in society.
    • They are not just language; they encompass the "social practices, identities, and values of the participants".
    • Gee uses the metaphor that Discourses use humans "to talk to, with, and against each other".
  • Intertextuality: A concept originally from literary studies referring to how texts influence, reference, or echo other texts.
    • Poststructuralists (Julia Kristeva & Roland Barthes): They expanded the concept to suggest all texts are intertextual because they draw from pre-existing cultural codes.
    • Norman Fairclough: Defines it as texts being "full of snatches of other texts" which may be assimilated, contradicted, or ironically echoed.
  • Dialogism (Mikhail Bakhtin): The theory that all language and thought are inherently "dialogic," meaning they are produced through interaction between differing voices rather than in isolation. No utterance can be understood without its context.
  • Heteroglossia (Mikhail Bakhtin): Closely related to dialogism, this refers to the diversity of voices, styles, and points of view within a language. It describes the "play of social languages" in society.
  • Songlines (Dreaming Tracks): An example of intertextuality in Australian Aboriginal culture. These are songs about paths across the landscape taken by "creator-beings" during the "Dreaming" (creation time). They serve as a guide for navigating the physical landscape and daily actions.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion (For "Discuss" Questions)

  1. The Historical Nature of Discourses

    Gee argues that Discourses are historical entities that exist before we are born and continue after we die.

    • The Argument: When we debate current issues, we are often channeling historical clashes between Discourses, even if we are unaware of the specific history.
    • The Example (Slavery in MA): Gee uses the 19th-century debate on escaped slaves in Massachusetts. This was a clash between two specific Discourses:
      • Transcendentalists (e.g., Emerson/Thoreau): Championed "higher law" and personal conscience over the state.
      • Elites: Championed the "rule of law" above personal conscience.
    • Significance: These historical themes (conscience vs. law) were handed down and shape modern Conversations today, such as debates on civil disobedience.
  2. Moral Citizenship and "Big C" Conversations

    Gee explicitly connects knowledge of Discourse to ethics.

    • The Argument: Being aware of the "Big C Conversations" that inform your society is a "necessary precondition for morally responsible citizenship".
    • Why: Just as you cannot fairly comment on a private conversation you haven't heard, you cannot fairly critique or vote on societal issues if you are unaware of the history, views, values, and identities behind the opposing viewpoints.
    • Current Context: Gee warns this is in short supply because our society is "highly polarized" and inhabited by echo chambers.
  3. Contextual Meaning: The "All Lives Matter" Case

    Gee argues that you cannot analyze a claim based only on its literal meaning; you must analyze its place within the "Big C Conversation."

    • The Case Study: The phrase "All lives matter".
    • Literal Meaning: On the surface, it appears to advocate for universal human rights.
    • Discourse Analysis: When viewed through the lens of the "Big C Conversation" regarding Black Lives Matter (which focuses on specific systemic racism), the phrase changes meaning.
    • The Conclusion: In context, the phrase is seen by many as a move to distract from or diminish the specific focus on Black suffering. Without understanding the Conversation, the claim cannot be fully understood.
  4. Intertextuality as "Identity Work"

    Gee discusses how we use intertextuality (referencing other texts) to give our mundane lives deeper meaning.

    • Pop Culture: We quote movies (like Groundhog Day or Shawshank Redemption) to bond over shared frustrations, turning a boring job into a shared cultural reference.
    • Biblical Typology: A deep form of intertextuality used by Jewish people, interpreting current life events through the lens of biblical stories (e.g., "Exile" and "Return") to find purpose and continuity.
    • The Problem of Modernity: Gee notes that modern cultures that have lost these deep mythological ties often struggle to give "deeper meaning" to mundane life.
Chapter 7

Applying the Tools

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Active Context: Unlike a static view where context is just "there" (the physical setting), active context is "actively created by speakers and writers". It is an integral part of the communicative process that is constantly being "negotiated and redefined by the participants".
  • Situational Meaning: The specific meaning a word takes on in a specific context, distinct from its literal dictionary definition. For example, "Life" can mean biological existence, a biography ("Her life in America"), or vitality ("Life of the party") depending on the situation.
  • Generative AI (in Discourse Analysis): Algorithms that generate new data similar to their training data. Gee uses this to define meaning as public: AI has no "intentions" or "mind," yet it produces meaning because meaning relies on shared social conventions, not on "mind reading".
  • Consensual Limits (Patricia Williams): A concept derived from legal theory referring to the "social and ethical" boundaries we place on what words mean. It suggests that defining a word is not a mechanical act but a "social negotiation and contestation" regarding authority and legitimacy.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Context is Actively Created (Not Passive)

    Gee argues against the idea that context is merely the backdrop of a conversation.

    • The Argument: Communicators do not just exist in a context; they "actively construe" (interpret) the physical setting and the other participants.
    • The Implication: We manipulate beliefs and expectations to change the context. Because we cannot know what is in another person's head (or even fully in our own unconscious minds), communication is always "active guessing work" to build a context that makes sense.
  2. The "Public" Nature of Meaning (The AI Argument)

    Gee reveals that the sample text in this chapter (a nuanced argument about abortion) was actually written by a Generative AI (Claude 3). He uses this to make a massive theoretical point about where meaning resides.

    • The Problem: We usually assume meaning comes from a speaker's intention/mind. AI has no mind, feelings, or intentions.
    • The Resolution: We can still interpret AI because meaning is not locked in a head; it is "public and social".
    • The Takeaway: Meaning comes from "social conventions that we share," which allows us to treat AI text as if it were human.
  3. The Politics of Definition (Patricia Williams’ Sausage)

    Gee cites legal scholar Patricia Williams to discuss how we define words when reality gets messy.

    • The Case: A sausage manufacturer argued that "pig meat plus impurities (rodents/sawdust)" still counted as "sausage" because it came out of a sausage machine.
    • The Discussion: Williams argues we have three choices when defining a word in a new context:
      • Validate the machine: If the machine made it, it's sausage (Enlarging the authority of the maker).
      • Expand the definition: "Sausage" now includes "rodent sausage".
      • Challenge Comprehension (The Ethical Choice): We must define the "consensual limits" of the word and acknowledge our own role in the "creation of reality".
    • Conclusion: Defining words is an ethical act where we decide "what the limits of their meanings will or should be".
  4. The "Blueprint" vs. "Ingredient" Metaphor

    Gee discusses the structural ambiguity of the phrase "X begins Y" (specifically in the debate "Life begins at conception").

    • The Ambiguity:
      • Meaning A (Precursor): "A journey begins with a map." The map is a start, but not part of the journey itself. (Metaphor: Blueprint).
      • Meaning B (Component): "A journey begins with a first step." The step is part of the journey. (Metaphor: First Chapter).
    • The Application: The debate over "Life begins at conception" is a debate over which grammar applies: Is the zygote a blueprint (precursor to a human) or the first chapter (already a human)?
Chapter 8

Basic Meanings, Situational Meanings, and Figured Worlds

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Basic Meaning: The general, literal meaning a word or grammatical structure has out of context.
    • Example: The basic meaning of "cat" is a feline animal. The basic meaning of a "Subject" is the topic of a sentence.
    • Validity: Analysis of basic meaning relies on the rules of grammar (syntax and semantics).
  • Situational Meaning: The highly specific meaning words and phrases take on in actual contexts of use.
    • Example: In a context about breakable objects, "The cat broke" means a statue, not an animal. In the sentence "Mistakes were made," the subject choice is used to evade responsibility.
  • The Frame Problem: The dilemma that context is "indefinitely large," and any new aspect of context we consider can change our interpretation of an utterance.
    • Double-edged sword: It is a problem because interpretations are always vulnerable to change, but a tool because widening the context allows analysts to see what is being left unsaid or effaced.
  • Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA): A form of analysis that goes beyond descriptive meaning to study how language is associated with "politics" (power, privilege, and the distribution of social goods).
  • Figured Worlds (Dorothy Holland): A socially constructed realm of interpretation in which "particular characters and actors are recognized, significance is assigned to certain acts, and particular outcomes are valued over others".
    • Simplified World: It is a simplified model (like a story/movie in our heads) populated by agents moved by specific forces.
  • Cultural Models: A shared set of beliefs, values, and norms that play a crucial role in defining what is considered "normal" or acceptable within a society.
  • Schema: A term from cognitive psychology referring to a mental framework that helps organize and interpret information (e.g., a "restaurant schema" helps you order food even in a new place).

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. The Politics of Grammar (The Gagnon Example)

    Gee argues that even simple grammatical choices (Basic Meanings) can do political work (Situational Meanings).

    • The Case: A historian (Gagnon) wrote: "Though the Whig and Tory parties were both narrowly confined to the privileged classes, they represented different factions."
    • The Analysis:
      • Grammar: Gagnon put the class issue ("confined to privileged classes") in a dependent clause. Dependent clauses function to express information that is assumed/taken for granted, not asserted.
      • Politics: By backgrounding the class issue, Gagnon "effaced" the argument that class conflict drives history. He forced the reader to accept the class divide as a mere background detail rather than the main point of debate.
    • Conclusion: Grammatical structure (what you foreground vs. background) determines what is up for debate and what is taken as "truth," which is a political act.
  2. The Frame Problem & Validity

    Gee discusses how we determine if an interpretation is valid given that context never ends.

    • The Argument: You cannot claim an analysis is valid unless you keep widening the context until the widening "appears to make no difference" to the interpretation.
    • Example (Child Mortality):
      • Narrow Context (Medical): "Children in Africa die because of malaria." (Valid: It is a medical fact).
      • Wide Context (Economic): Malaria only kills vulnerable populations due to poverty and lack of infrastructure. In this context, "because of malaria" is misleading; they die "because of poverty".
    • Discussion: A narrow context hides political realities; widening the frame reveals them.
  3. Why "Definitions" Fail (The Pope is a Bachelor)

    Gee argues that we do not use words based on their dictionary definitions, but based on "Typical Stories" (Cultural Models).

    • The Puzzle: A "bachelor" is defined as an unmarried man. The Pope is an unmarried man. Yet, we are reluctant to call the Pope a bachelor.
    • The Reason: Our cultural model of a "bachelor" is a story about a man who is eligible for marriage but has chosen to wait. The Pope does not fit this "typical story" because he has vowed never to marry.
    • The Takeaway: We process language through "Social Mind Frameworks" (stories about what is normal), not just logic.
  4. The Evolution of Figured Worlds (The University)

    Gee uses the University to show how "Figured Worlds" (identities and roles) change over time.

    • Old Figured World: A place where students (seeking knowledge) and professors (creating knowledge) interact physically.
    • New Figured World: A place where students seek credentials (not just learning), faculty are often casual employees (gig workers), and learning is standardized/online.
    • Significance: "Reality" is not static; it is constructed by these shared stories. When the story changes, the institution itself changes.
Chapter 9

Figured Worlds

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Figured Worlds (Expanded): Simplified, often unconscious theories or stories about how the world works that mediate between the "micro" level of social interaction and the "macro" level of institutions.
    • Function: They are "first thoughts" or taken-for-granted assumptions about what is typical or normal.
  • Mental Simulations: The cognitive process where our minds run "movies" or "video games" to understand the present and prepare for the future.
    • Prototypical Simulations: The simulation we run when we assume a situation is "typical" (e.g., a standard wedding).
    • Special-Case Simulations: The simulation we adapt when we encounter a specific context that doesn't fit the prototype (e.g., a wedding between two men).
  • Cultivation Model (Annette Lareau): A parenting figured world (typical of the middle/upper class) where the child is treated like a plant that must be constantly monitored and tended. It involves negotiation, structured activities, and "book language".
  • Natural Growth Model (Annette Lareau): A parenting figured world (typical of the working class) where the child is treated like a plant that will develop naturally if given food and love. It involves less intervention, more free time, and directives rather than negotiation.
  • Types of Figured Worlds (Claudia Strauss):
    • Espoused Worlds: Theories we consciously say we believe.
    • Evaluative Worlds: Theories we use to judge ourselves or others.
    • Worlds-in-(inter)action: Theories that actually guide our actions.
  • Involution (Related to "Lying Flat"): A sociological term referring to the phenomenon where a social or cultural formation stagnates or cannot be transformed into a higher-level formation after it has developed to a certain stage. (Used by Chinese youth to describe their economy).
  • "Lying Flat" (Tang Ping): A figured world emerging among young Chinese people as a form of passive resistance against hyper-competition and overwork. It reinterprets "success" not as material wealth but as living quietly and finding personal value.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Simulations: How the Mind Works

    Gee argues that we do not store static definitions in our heads; we store simulations.

    • The Argument: Our minds are like video game engines. When we hear a word (like "Wedding"), we run a simulation based on our past experiences.
    • Foregrounding/Backgrounding: These simulations are not neutral. They foreground what we think is important (e.g., the bride's happiness) and background other things, just like a scientist's computer model.
    • The Danger: We often mistake our "Prototypical Simulation" for what is "Right." When reality deviates from our simulation (e.g., a gay marriage), we often judge it as "deviant" rather than just "different".
  2. All Meaning is Local (The Mayan Example)

    Gee uses a case study from the Yucatan to prove that you cannot understand meaning without understanding the local Figured World.

    • The Case: A woman says a man is "drinking" (even though he is eating a roll) and "seated" (even though he might be standing).
    • The Figured World: In this Mayan culture:
      • "Drinking" refers to the time of day (morning/evening meals), not the action.
      • "Seated" means "at home and available," not the physical posture.
    • Conclusion: Meaning is not in the dictionary; it is "wedded to local, on-site social and Discourse practices".
  3. Parenting and Social Class (Lareau & Harkness)

    Gee discusses how different social classes operate in different Figured Worlds, creating social consequences.

    • Inconsistency (Harkness Study): Parents use the "Independence Model" inconsistently. A child refusing a diaper change is seen as "independent" (good), but the same child submitting to a nap in a car seat is not seen as "dependent" (bad). Parents only label "problematic" behaviors as "stages".
    • Outcome (Lareau Study):
      • Cultivation Model children gain skills valued by schools (negotiation, vocabulary) but can be entitled.
      • Natural Growth Model children are respectful and self-sustaining but may struggle with institutional authority.
    • The Politics: Schools assume the Cultivation Model is the norm. Therefore, they treat "Natural Growth" children as deficient, even though they are just different.
  4. Figured Worlds in Conflict (The "Colonized" Mind)

    Gee uses Claudia Strauss's study of working-class men to show how we can hold conflicting models that hurt us.

    • The Conflict: These men held two models:
      • Success Model (Evaluative): "Anyone can succeed if they work hard" (The American Dream).
      • Breadwinner Model (Action): "Family comes first" (e.g., refusing overtime to be with kids).
    • The Consequence: The men acted on the Breadwinner model (being good fathers) but judged themselves by the Success model.
    • Result: They felt like failures (low self-esteem) because they were "colonized" by a figured world (Success) that didn't actually fit their lived reality or actions.
Chapter 10

More on Figured Worlds

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Politics (in Discourse Analysis): In Gee's framework, politics refers to anything (talk, text, interaction) where "social goods" are at stake.
    • Social Goods: Things like power, status, valued knowledge, positions, or possessions. Figured worlds are inherently political because they embed assumptions about what is "appropriate" or "normal," thereby distributing these goods.
  • Reproduction of Cultural Capital: A sociological theory (often associated with Bourdieu) explaining how class privilege is maintained.
    • Mechanism: Middle-class homes socialize children in ways that "resonate" with school practices. Schools treat these home-based habits as "natural" or "giftedness," rewarding them.
    • Result: Advantage is passed down not just through money, but through values and habits (cultural capital) that are compounded in school.
  • Deep Canvasing: A political method of engaging people in conversation to change their minds on identity-heavy issues (e.g., immigration).
    • Method: Instead of arguing with facts/logic, the canvasser asks the person to recall a specific personal experience (a "lived memory") to trigger empathy and emotional reflection.
    • Goal: To bypass abstract categories ("immigrants are bad") and trigger a simulation of a specific human ("Susie, my neighbor").

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. The Inconsistency of Figured Worlds (The Marcella Case)

    Gee analyzes an interview with "Marcella" (a Latina student) to show how figured worlds are often partial, inconsistent, and contradictory.

    • The Contradiction:
      • Theory A (Cultural Capital): Marcella argues that white people succeed because they "get more education" and have parents who teach them the "points" needed for a career (a structural/sociological view).
      • Theory B (Biological Determinism): She simultaneously argues that white people are "born smart" or have a "piece of smart" inside them.
    • The "Colonized" Mind (Again): Just like the working-class men in Chapter 9, Marcella uses a dominant figured world (biological intelligence) that works against her own interests, mitigating the power of her own sociological insight.
  2. Clashing Frameworks (The Middleton Project)

    Gee describes a failed collaboration between university Professors and middle-school Teachers to show what happens when figured worlds clash without reflection.

    • The Conflict:
      • Professors' World: Focused on Diversity (race/class), macro-level power structures, and victimhood. They viewed the "downtown" as unsafe and wanted students to study their segregated neighborhoods.
      • Teachers' World: Focused on Commonality (citizenship), individual agency, and nurturing. They viewed the "downtown" as the melting pot where everyone becomes a "Middleton citizen."
    • The Result: They used the same words (like "teacher") to mean different things and viewed each other with suspicion (Professors saw teachers as racist; Teachers saw professors as arrogant) because they never made their figured worlds explicit.
  3. The Power of Experience (Deep Canvasing)

    Gee argues that you cannot change a person's mind on political issues using logic if that issue is tied to their identity.

    • The Argument: Humans view others in two ways: as Abstract Categories (e.g., "Immigrant") or as Contextualized Individuals (e.g., "Susie").
    • The Mechanism: To change a mind, you must trigger a simulation. Facts don't trigger simulations; experiences do.
    • Quote: "It is hard to rail against immigrants when Susie pops into your mind and heart."
  4. Figured Worlds as Tools of Inquiry (The Assumption of Sense)

    Gee concludes by offering a "moral principle" for Discourse Analysis.

    • The Principle: We must assume that everyone has "good reasons" and makes "deep sense" within their own socio-cultural framework.
    • Why: Humans are "sense-makers." If someone sounds illogical (like Marcella or the Teachers), it is usually because the analyst does not yet understand the specific Figured World they are operating in.
Chapter 11

Context

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Reflexivity (in Context): The property of language where it simultaneously reflects the context (what is historically/physically there) and constructs it.
    • Metaphor: Language and context are like "two mirrors facing each other," endlessly reflecting.
    • Example: Speaking to someone in a friendly way reflects the friendship but also constructs the relationship as friendly in that moment.
  • Relevance (of Context): Context is indefinitely large, but communicators use only the "relevant parts" to interpret meaning.
    • Mechanism: Speakers/writers use "cues" (grammar, gestures) to signal what part of the context is relevant (e.g., pointing to an orange rubber duck makes the duck relevant; otherwise, it is just background noise).
  • Intertextuality (Expanded Definition): In this chapter, Gee expands the definition to include the mixing or juxtaposing of different Social Languages (associated with different Discourses) within a single text.
  • Hybridity (Social Languages): The mixing of different social languages to assemble a transformed or novel social language for a specific context.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. The "Chicken and Egg" of Context

    Gee argues that Context is not passive; it is active and reflexive.

    • The Argument: We often ask: Does the situation dictate the language, or does the language create the situation? Gee answers: Both.
    • The Logic: Language reflects reality (e.g., "How ya doin'" reflects a casual workplace), but it also construes reality (e.g., if you said "What's your problem?" you would construe the context as hostile). You cannot separate the two.
  2. Situational Meaning & Figured Worlds (The Democracy Case)

    Gee argues that you cannot understand the Situational Meaning of a word without knowing the Figured World behind it.

    • The Case: Milton Friedman's quote: "Restrictions on economic freedom would turn out to be inconsistent with democracy."
    • The Paradox: The dictionary definition of Democracy (voting) allows people to vote for restrictions. So how is it inconsistent?
    • The Resolution: You must know the Neo-Liberal Figured World (where freedom from coercion is paramount). In that world, voting away economic freedom destroys the "consent of the governed," thus destroying democracy. Without the world, the sentence is nonsense.
  3. Conflict in Discourse (The Middleton "Small Talk")

    Gee revisits the conflict between Sara (University Professor) and the Teachers to show how Discourses clash even in small talk.

    • The Interaction: The group engages in small talk about local history.
    • The Teachers: Use the Middleton Discourse (bonding over shared oral history/memories of the town).
    • Sara: Uses Academic Discourse mixed with everyday language (citing a student's research paper as evidence).
    • The Clash: Sara tries to bond, but her Discourse privileges written research over oral memory, which unintentionally clashes with the teachers' values, replicating the tension of the formal meeting.
  4. Intertextuality as a Trace of Conversation (The Burrito Case)

    Gee analyzes an essay by Richard Ruiz to show how texts are "traces" of Big C Conversations.

    • The Text: Ruiz writes about a legal case deciding if a Burrito is a Sandwich. He mixes social languages (Linguistics, Vernacular Spanish, Business Talk, Religious Talk).
    • The "Big C" Conversation: The essay isn't really about burritos. It is a trace of the historical debate about Cultural Competence in Law (referencing cases like Plessy v. Ferguson where judges made culturally incompetent decisions about race). The "Burrito" is a proxy for the rights of Mexican culture in the US.
Chapter 12

Transcription, an “Ideal” Discourse Analysis, and Validity

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Transcription: A theoretical entity, not a raw record of reality.
    • Nature: Speech always has more detail than can be captured. A transcript is based on "theoretical judgments" about what is relevant to the analysis. It does not stand outside the analysis; it is part of it.
  • The "Ideal" Discourse Analysis: A hypothetical, fully complete analysis that asks 42 Questions.
    • The Math: 6 Tools of Inquiry (Situational Meanings, Social Languages, Figured Worlds, Intertextuality, Discourses, Conversations) × 7 Building Tasks = 42 Questions.
    • Reality: No real analysis does all 42; we pick what is relevant, but the others remain as "unfinished background."
  • Validity (in Discourse Analysis): Validity is not about "reflecting reality" (since reality is interpreted). It is about the analysis being "trustworthy." It is social and cumulative.
    The Four Elements of Validity:
    • Convergence: Do answers to the different questions (tools/building tasks) support the same conclusion?
    • Agreement: Do "native speakers" of the Discourse or other analysts agree the analysis is plausible?
    • Coverage: Can the analysis explain related data or predict what happens in similar situations?
    • Linguistic Details: Is the analysis tightly tied to specific grammatical devices and structures?

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Transcription is Theory (The "Light" Case Study)

    Gee uses an interaction between a Researcher and an African American student to show how transcription details change the meaning.

    • The Context: A researcher asks a girl about light. The girl starts talking about dinosaurs dying.
    • The Misunderstanding: The researcher thinks the girl is off-topic and confused.
    • The Analysis (The Details):
      • Intonation: The girl uses "low pitch" to signal obviousness and "high pitch/emphasis" to signal new/significant info.
      • Topic Chaining: The girl uses a specific discourse style (background first: dinosaurs died because sun was blocked -> topic: light).
    • Conclusion: The researcher "co-constructed" the misunderstanding by failing to recognize the child's logic. A detailed transcript reveals the child was making sense, but the adult's "school-based" expectations blinded her to it.
  2. Validity is Social

    Gee argues that validity is not an individual achievement but a social process.

    • The Argument: No single analysis is perfect. Validity is established over time through "social judgments and adjudications" by the community of scholars.
    • The Goal: We do not seek "Final Truth." We seek an analysis that explains the data so well that any future investigation of the same data would have to take it seriously.
Chapter 13

Processing and Organizing Language

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Speech Spurts (Idea Units / Tone Units / Lines): Thanks to the biological limits of the human brain and vocal system, speech is produced in small spurts, usually one clause long.
    1. Function: Each spurt represents one focus of consciousness.
    2. Mind's Eye Metaphor: Just as the eye fixates on one small part of a scene at a time, the mind focuses on one small piece of information at a time.
  • Stanza: A larger unit of speech made up of a "clump" of lines that deal with a single topic, event, image, perspective, or theme.
    1. Structure: When the character, time, or event changes, a new Stanza begins. Stanzas function like "boxes" holding related lines.
  • Focus Stress: The most prominent level of stress in a line (intonation contour).
    1. Rule: In English, the focus stress typically falls on the last content word (marking all new info). If it falls elsewhere, it marks a specific contrast.
  • New vs. Old Information:
    1. New Information: Unpredictable info (stressed).
    2. Old Information: Predictable or previously mentioned info (unstressed/backgrounded).
  • Macro-Structure: The larger "body parts" of a narrative or argument. For a story, these are typically:
    1. Setting: Time/Place/Characters.
    2. Catalyst: The problem/event that starts the action.
    3. Crisis: The peak of the problem.
    4. Evaluation: Why the story is interesting/tellable.
    5. Resolution: How the problem is solved.
    6. Coda: Closing the story.
  • Macro-Lines: "Sentences" in speech. While children often speak in single clauses, adults use syntax to stitch multiple micro-lines (intonation units) together into larger, looser "sentences" or macro-lines.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. The Physics of Speech (The "Spurt" Theory)

    Gee argues that the way we speak is physically determined by our biology.

    • The Argument: Speech is not a continuous stream; it is a series of "spurts" (lines).
    • The Connection: Each line corresponds to a single "idea unit." This proves that our consciousness processes the world in small, bite-sized chunks, not as a seamless whole.
  2. The Logic of Intonation (Stress = Meaning)

    Gee argues that stress is not random; it is the primary way we indicate information status.

    • The Rule: We stress New Information and leave Old Information unstressed.
    • Example:
      • "Did your daughter find a job?"
      • "She found a job as a SECRETARY." (Secretary is new; job is old).
    • Significance: If you miss the stress, you miss the point. A change in stress changes the meaning (e.g., "I LOVE Mary" vs. "I love Mary").
  3. The Structure of Narrative (The "Puppy" Story)

    Gee analyzes a 7-year-old's story to show that even children use complex cognitive structures (Stanzas).

    • The Organization: The child didn't just ramble. She organized her speech into distinct Stanzas (Setting -> Catalyst -> Crisis).
    • Cultural Specificity: The child placed the Evaluation before the Resolution (common in some African American storytelling traditions), whereas adults often spread evaluation throughout.
    • Parallelism: The child used parallel structures (describing the Puppy vs. describing the Father) to create a thematic conflict between "Nature/Freedom" (Puppy) and "Culture/Discipline" (Father).
  4. Macro-lines and Adult Complexity

    Gee distinguishes between child and adult speech organization.

    • Child Speech: Often one line = one clause.
    • Adult Speech: More complex. Adults use syntax (subordinating clauses, "and," "which") to weave multiple lines into Macro-lines.
    • Implication: In speech, "sentences" (macro-lines) are emergent and loose, often constructed on the fly, unlike written sentences which are tightly integrated.
Chapter 14

Meaning and Choice

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Thematic Meaning: A third level of meaning (deeper than Basic and Situational meaning) that connects to themes of culture, history, identity, and institutions.
    • Nature: It is often implicit and tied to values or rituals (e.g., family vs. commodity).
  • Grammar as Choice: Grammar is not just a set of rules but a set of choices that allow speakers/writers to take a specific perspective on reality.
    • Example: "John married Mary" (John's perspective) vs. "Mary married John" (Mary's perspective).
  • Topic vs. Comment:
    • Topic (Subject): What the clause is about.
    • Comment (Predicate): What is said about the topic. Choosing a different topic changes the perspective of the sentence.
  • Asserted vs. Assumed Information:
    • Asserted (Independent Clause): Foregrounded info that is open to debate.
    • Assumed (Dependent Clause): Backgrounded info that the reader is expected to take for granted.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Meaning is Choice (The "Bloodied" News Report)

    Gee analyzes a news headline to show how word choice constructs reality.

    • The Headline: "A white nationalist was bloodied Saturday while confronting protestors."
    • The Choices:
      • Word Choice: "Bloodied" implies violence/war (vs. "cut" or "injured").
      • Topic Choice: Making the "white nationalist" the topic (subject) focuses on his experience, not the protestors' actions.
      • Assumed Info: Putting the confrontation in a dependent clause ("while confronting...") makes the conflict background information, not the main point of debate.
  2. The Social Contract of Grammar

    Gee argues that grammar creates a social contract between writer and reader.

    • The Contract: Readers are expected to challenge Asserted info but accept Assumed info.
    • Manipulation: Writers can manipulate this by hiding controversial claims in dependent clauses (e.g., "Trump was shouted down when he was telling the truth" forces the reader to accept "he was telling the truth" as background fact).
  3. AI vs. Human Meaning (The Leona Story)

    Gee compares human interpretation with ChatGPT-4 using a story by "Leona" (an African American child).

    • The Story: Leona tells a story about her grandmother baking cakes at home vs. buying cakes at a bakery.
    • AI's Interpretation: Focuses on Basic/Situational Meaning (Family love overcoming bad luck). It misses the deeper point.
    • Human Interpretation (Thematic Meaning): The story is about Signs and Symbols.
      • Home Cake: A symbol of kinship and love.
      • Bakery Cake: A commodity/duplicitous symbol.
    • Theme: The grandmother loses her human status (growls like a dog) because she mistook a commodity for a symbol of love.
    • Conclusion: AI understands definitions and contexts but (currently) misses the deep Thematic Meanings tied to human history and identity.
Chapter 15

Sample of Discourse Analysis 1

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • I-Statements: A tool of inquiry used to analyze identity by categorizing how a speaker refers to themselves.
    • Cognitive: "I think," "I know" (Thinking/Knowing).
    • Affective: "I want," "I like" (Desiring/Liking).
    • State/Action: "I am mature," "I hit him" (Being/Doing).
    • Ability/Constraint: "I can't," "I have to" (Possibility/Necessity).
    • Achievement: "I want to go to MIT" (Mainstream success).
  • Habitus (Bourdieu): One's habitual way of being in the world as an embodied social being. It is an ingrained trait or disposition (e.g., knowing how to act in a middle-class setting without thinking about it).
  • Co-Construction (of Identity): The principle that identities are not solo performances; they are mutually built by the speaker and the listener (interviewer).
    • Example: An interviewer asks abstract questions to a Professor (constructing a "Global Voice") but local questions to a Teacher (constructing a "Local Voice").

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Class and Identity Building (Working Class vs. Upper Middle Class)

    Gee compares interviews with Working Class (WC) and Upper Middle Class (UMC) teens to show how they build different worlds.

    • Working Class Teens (e.g., Jeremy, Maria):
      • Worldview: Immersed in the "Lifeworld" (everyday social/dialogic interaction).
      • I-Statements: High in Affective and State/Action statements.
      • Logic: Their statements are dialogic (responses to ongoing arguments with parents/peers).
    • Upper Middle Class Teens (e.g., Brian, Karin):
      • Worldview: Focused on "Achievement Space" and biographical trajectories (future success).
      • I-Statements: High in Cognitive and Achievement statements.
      • Logic: Their statements are abstract assessments/evaluations of their own success.
  2. The "Restricted Code" Irony

    Gee challenges Bernstein's idea that working-class speech is "restricted."

    • The Irony:
      • WC Teens: Speak personally about diversity because they live it (diverse neighborhoods). Their code appears "restricted" but is grounded in rich experience.
      • UMC Teens: Speak abstractly/distantly about diversity because they lack experience with it (segregated suburbs). Their code appears "elaborated" (complex syntax) but is actually a way to "cloak" their lack of experience and personal anxiety about achievement.
  3. Narrative as Identity Work (The "Brian" Case)

    Gee analyzes a story by Brian (UMC teen) to show how narratives rationalize identity.

    • The Story: Brian tells a story about being a "spaz" (outsider) who got angry when people cheated, but then decided to "stop" and became normal.
    • The Theme: A "redemptive moment" where Rationality overcomes Emotion.
    • The Identity: Brian constructs a Middle-Class Identity: one must suppress emotion (anger at unfairness) to succeed in competition. This explains why he is less passionate about social justice (racism) later in the interview—he has trained himself to ignore unfairness to "win."
Chapter 16

Sample of Discourse Analysis 2

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Motifs: Recurring themes, images, or ideas that "co-locate" (correlate) throughout a text, giving it coherence and texture.
    • Sandra's Motifs: Disconnection (things don't connect), Not Caring (indifference as a defense), and Language/Laughter (noise/affect as resistance).
  • The Principle of the Echo: A narrative device where later parts of a text mirror or repeat earlier parts (common in oral storytelling).
    • Example: In Sandra's story, the boyfriend's laughter in the first sub-story is echoed by the sister's sigh in the second.
  • Restoration of a Lack/Loss: A classic narrative closing device where something lost (e.g., a table) is returned or restored, resolving the conflict.
  • Parallelism (Equivalence Logic): Structuring a text so that different parts (stanzas) mirror each other in structure or content, creating meaning through comparison.
    • Function: It equates different events (e.g., breaking a fan and breaking a table) to show they share the same underlying logic.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Sandra's Worldview (The Three Motifs)

    Gee analyzes the interview of "Sandra" (a working-class teen) to reveal her specific Figured World.

    • The Motifs:
      • Disconnection: Actions have no consequences (boyfriend doesn't get punished).
      • Not Caring: A stance of indifference to authority and judgment.
      • Language/Laughter: A preference for affective, noisy, non-sense language over "authoritative" language.
    • The Argument: Sandra constructs a world where "Authoritative Representation" (facts/truth spoken by adults/schools) is hurtful and "stupid," while "Sympathetic Social Interaction" (laughter/affect) is the only thing that heals.
  2. Validity through Convergence (Motifs + Narrative)

    Gee uses this chapter to demonstrate Validity.

    • The Method: He shows that the themes found in the entire interview (Motifs) converge with the themes found in the specific narrative ("The Return of the Table").
    • Result: Because the broad data and the specific narrative support the same conclusion (Sandra's rejection of authority), the analysis has high validity (Coverage).
  3. The Narrative Structure ("The Return of the Table")

    Gee analyzes Sandra's story to show it is not a rambling mess but a tightly structured oral narrative.

    • Structure: Two parallel sub-stories about "Breaking."
    • Sub-story 1 (Loss): Boyfriend breaks a fan (accident/play) -> Laughes -> No consequence.
    • Sub-story 2 (Gain/Restoration): Boyfriend breaks himself (accident/injury) -> Gets money -> Restores the table unasked.
    • Theme: A world of accidents, play, and restoration where "authoritative commands" (Mother saying "Stop") are ignored, but affective bonds remain.
  4. Identity as Opposition to School

    Gee concludes that Sandra's identity work is fundamentally at odds with the institution of School.

    • The Conflict: Schools value Authoritative Representation (facts, truth, rules). Sandra values Sympathetic Interaction (caring, laughter, noise).
    • The Outcome: Unless the school adapts to understand her "Caring Model," she will disaffiliate from education, viewing it as "uncaring" and "making no sense."
Chapter 17

Multimodality

Part 1: Key Definitions

  • Multimodality: The use of different modes of communication (words, images, music, gesture, movement) within a single text to create meaning.
    • Examples: Opera, Video Games, Textbooks with diagrams, Face-to-Face conversation (which uses gesture/gaze).
  • Mode (Gunther Kress): A "socially and culturally shaped resource for making meaning."
    1. The 5 Modes (New London Group): Visual, Linguistic, Spatial, Aural, and Gestural.
  • Sensual Constructions: A concept Gee introduces to describe multimodal symbols that blend Sensation with Meaning.
    • Definition: Experienced clusters of sensation and feeling that take on deep situational meaning over time.
    • Example: The Madeleine Cookie (Proust) or the Red Scarf (Attack on Titan). It is not just an object; it is a multisensory experience that triggers memory/theme.
  • System of Systems (Video Games): Gee defines video games as a complex multimodal system composed of two melded systems:
    • The Game World: The fictional world the player sees/moves in (Graphics, Action).
    • The User Interface: The HUD, menus, and maps that communicate game data to the player.

Part 2: Core Arguments & Discussion

  1. Sensation and Situational Meaning (Proust & Anime)

    Gee argues that Multimodality allows sensation to ground language.

    • The Case Studies:
      • Proust's Cookie: The taste/texture of the madeleine is a sensory trigger for "Involuntary Memory." The cookie becomes a Sensual Construction exploring time and memory.
      • Attack on Titan's Red Scarf: A visual/narrative symbol. Initially, it means "warmth/safety" (Eren saving Mikasa). Over the series, it accumulates meanings of "loyalty," "loss," and finally "letting go."
    • The Point: Meaning is not just verbal definitions; it is built through recurring sensory experiences.
  2. Discourse in Video Games (Metal Gear Solid)

    Gee analyzes Metal Gear Solid to show how games create a unique Discourse.

    • The Discourse: The "Solid Snake Discourse" involves specific:
      • Saying: Philosophical dialog mixed with technical military jargon.
      • Doing: Stealth activities (sneaking, hiding), not just shooting.
      • Being: An identity of a weary, ethical soldier.
    • The Melding: Games are unique because they meld two Discourses: the Character's (Snake) and the Player's (me playing Snake). The player must enact the identity (be a "good Snake") to succeed.
    • Key Slide Addition (Radar Map): The professor highlighted the "Radar Map" in MGS as an example of how function determines mode.
      • Mode 1 (Simple Screen): It looks like a simple 2D game.
      • Mode 2 (UI Element): In the context of the 3D game, it functions as a "User Interface" allowing the player to look through walls. The same visual has different modes depending on its function.
    • Specific Modalities in MGS (Slide List): The slides explicitly list these 9 modes for MGS:
      • Graphics
      • Written Language
      • Spoken Language
      • Characters/Actions/Environments
      • Game Mechanics
      • Sound
      • Music
      • Haptic (Touch)
      • User Interface
  3. Multimodality and "Thematic Meaning"

    Gee connects this back to Chapter 14.

    • The Argument: Just as words can have deep Thematic Meaning (values/identity), so can images and actions in multimodal texts.
    • Example: The "Red Scarf" isn't just a red object; it carries the Thematic Meaning of human connection in a cruel world.
    • Conclusion: Discourse Analysis applies to all meaningful human communication, not just speech, because "saying, being, and doing always swim in a sea of human sensations."
